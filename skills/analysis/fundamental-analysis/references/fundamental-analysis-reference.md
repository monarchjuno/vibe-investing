## Objective and mindset

Use fundamental analysis to estimate a company's intrinsic value and compare it with the market price. The main goal is not to predict the next price move. The main goal is to judge whether the business is financially sound, strategically durable, and reasonably priced for a long-term investor.

Use it to support:

- Finding potentially undervalued companies
- Making long-term investment decisions
- Understanding business quality and financial stability
- Managing downside risk by avoiding weak balance sheets and fragile earnings

## Financial statement checklist

### Income statement

Review:

- Revenue growth trend
- Gross margin, operating margin, and net margin
- Earnings consistency
- One-time gains or losses

Ask:

- Is growth steady, cyclical, or deteriorating?
- Are margins stable, improving, or compressing?
- Are reported profits supported by core operations?

### Balance sheet

Review:

- Cash and equivalents
- Receivables and inventory
- Total debt and debt maturity pressure
- Shareholders' equity
- Working capital position

Ask:

- Is the company liquid enough to handle short-term obligations?
- Is leverage rising faster than the business is improving?
- Are assets productive and credible, or potentially overstated?

### Cash flow statement

Review:

- Operating cash flow
- Capital expenditure
- Free cash flow direction
- Reliance on financing activities

Ask:

- Do profits convert into cash?
- Is growth being funded internally or by constant external financing?
- Is cash generation strong enough to support debt service and reinvestment?

## Core metrics and interpretation

### PER

Formula:

- Price per share / earnings per share

Use it to judge how much the market is paying for current earnings.

Interpret carefully:

- A low PER can suggest undervaluation, but it can also signal weak growth, poor earnings quality, or a cyclical profit peak.
- A high PER can reflect overvaluation, but it can also reflect strong expected growth.

### PBR

Formula:

- Market price / book value per share

Use it to judge how the market values the company's net assets.

Interpret carefully:

- A low PBR can indicate undervaluation, especially in asset-heavy businesses.
- A low PBR can also reflect poor returns on assets, asset impairment risk, or weak business quality.
- PBR is less informative for businesses where intangible assets and network effects matter more than book value.

### ROE

Formula:

- Net income / average shareholders' equity

Use it to judge how effectively management uses shareholder capital.

Interpret carefully:

- A high ROE often signals strong profitability and capital efficiency.
- A high ROE can be misleading if it is driven mainly by heavy leverage or shrinking equity.

### Debt-related measures

Use debt ratio, debt-to-equity, interest coverage, or similar leverage measures to judge solvency and financial resilience.

Interpret carefully:

- High leverage can amplify returns in good periods and destroy flexibility in weak periods.
- Always connect leverage analysis to cash generation, refinancing risk, and cyclicality.

### Liquidity measures

Use the current ratio, quick ratio, and working capital trend to judge short-term financial flexibility.

Interpret carefully:

- Weak liquidity can become a serious problem even when the income statement looks acceptable.
- Improving liquidity is supportive, but only when it is not created by temporary balance-sheet window dressing.

## Industry, management, and risk

Do not stop at company-only analysis. Evaluate the business inside its market context.

Review:

- Industry growth rate and structure
- Competitive position and pricing power
- Management quality and execution record
- Capital allocation discipline
- Regulatory, legal, macro, and technology risks

Ask:

- Does the company operate in a favorable industry?
- Does management have a credible strategy and a record of execution?
- What could permanently impair earnings power or balance-sheet strength?

## Value versus growth framing

Use the following distinction as a guide, not a rigid rule.

### Value characteristics

- Market price looks low relative to intrinsic value
- Multiples are modest relative to assets or earnings
- Business may be mature but financially stable
- Dividend support is more common

### Growth characteristics

- Revenue and earnings have meaningful room to expand
- The company is reinvesting for future scale
- The market may already price in part of that future growth
- R&D, new products, or new markets matter more than current cheapness

Many companies combine both profiles. Prefer a nuanced conclusion over a forced label.

## Common red flags

- Revenue rises while operating cash flow weakens
- Net income looks strong because of one-time gains
- ROE is high mainly because leverage is high
- PER is low because earnings are temporarily inflated
- PBR is low because asset quality is questionable
- Debt grows faster than earnings power
- Management narrative is ambitious but unsupported by results

## Limits of the method

Remember the main limitations:

- Fundamental analysis relies heavily on historical and current data
- Sudden market, policy, regulatory, or management changes can break the thesis quickly
- It is stronger for medium- and long-term judgments than for short-term timing

Use price action, macro context, or technical analysis only as complementary context when the task requires it.
